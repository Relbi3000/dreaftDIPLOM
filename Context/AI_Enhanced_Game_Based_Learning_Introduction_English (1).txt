Development of an Application for AI-Enhanced Game-Based Learning
Group: SE-2326
Team: Telgarina Kuralay, Baimurzin Bakhtiyar, Bimurzin Assanali
Introduction
      The rapid digitalization of education has created new opportunities for interactive and personalized learning, but it has also exposed persistent problems that traditional instructional formats and many standard e-learning tools do not fully solve. In many educational settings, students still encounter repetitive exercises, delayed feedback, and one-size-fits-all learning sequences that do not respond well to differences in prior knowledge, pace, motivation, or learning preferences. As a result, even digital platforms can reproduce the same disengagement that appears in conventional instruction. This challenge makes it necessary to move beyond static content delivery and toward systems that can both motivate learners and adapt to their individual progress [1][3][17].
      One of the most influential responses to this challenge has been the use of gamification and game-based learning. Although these concepts are related, they are not identical. Gamification refers to the integration of game elements such as points, levels, badges, leaderboards, progress bars, and rewards into non-game learning contexts, whereas game-based learning uses game activity itself as the main vehicle for learning. The distinction is important for this thesis because the topic is not simply about adding decorative mechanics to an existing platform. Rather, it concerns the development of an application in which the learning process is structured through game-based interaction and enhanced by artificial intelligence so that the experience becomes adaptive, data-informed, and pedagogically purposeful [1][2][10].
      The literature provides strong evidence that well-designed gameful learning environments can improve motivation, engagement, and, under appropriate conditions, learning outcomes. Critical reviews and meta-analyses show that these effects are real but highly dependent on design quality, duration, learning context, and the degree to which game mechanics are aligned with educational objectives. In other words, game elements are effective when they support retrieval practice, feedback, mastery progression, and meaningful challenge, but they are much less effective when they function as superficial rewards detached from the learning task itself [1][3][14].
      Studies of widely used classroom platforms such as Kahoot! and Quizizz help illustrate both the promise and the limitations of current game-based educational tools. Research shows that such platforms often increase classroom participation, enjoyment, and attention by introducing immediate feedback, competition, visible progress, and replayable short tasks. At the same time, these tools are usually limited in their adaptivity. They often present the same questions or activity structures to all learners, rely on fixed difficulty, and provide only limited personalization beyond pacing or score displays. This means they are useful baseline examples, but they do not yet represent the full potential of AI-enhanced game-based learning applications [4][5][6][8][9].
      This limitation is precisely where artificial intelligence becomes important. Research on adaptive game-based learning and AI-enabled adaptive learning systems shows that personalization can be implemented at several levels: content selection, difficulty adjustment, pacing, hint generation, navigation paths, learner modeling, and recommendation of next actions. Instead of treating all users identically, an AI-enhanced application can interpret interaction data and modify the experience according to the learner's current needs. Such a design is especially valuable in educational games, where the balance between challenge and skill strongly influences engagement and persistence [10][11].
      From a software and system design perspective, this adaptive capability requires more than a game interface. It requires a structured architecture in which pedagogical logic, learner data, game mechanics, and AI services work together. Research on intelligent tutoring systems is useful here because it highlights key components that can inform the application design: a learner model, a domain or content model, a pedagogical model that determines instructional actions, and an interaction layer through which the learner experiences the system. Work on gamified intelligent tutoring systems further shows that game mechanics and adaptive tutoring should not be designed separately; they need to be integrated conceptually so that reward systems, progression, and feedback remain consistent with the educational model [12][17].
      Another major advantage of AI-enhanced game-based applications is the possibility of embedded analytics and stealth assessment. In such systems, evidence of learning can be inferred from gameplay behaviors, response patterns, choices, errors, time on task, or strategy use without interrupting the flow of play. This makes it possible to support formative assessment in a way that feels natural to learners while also giving teachers or system administrators richer data about progress. Recent work on learning analytics in serious games and on game-based assessment shows that interaction traces can support not only performance measurement, but also application improvement, personalization, and more timely instructional feedback [13][15][16][18].
      However, the literature also makes clear that building an effective educational application in this domain is not only a technical problem. It is equally a design problem and a user experience problem. User-centered design remains essential because students and teachers do not engage with educational technologies in identical ways. Learners differ in competitiveness, motivation, tolerance for public comparison, and preferred modes of interaction. In addition, poor usability can undermine even a theoretically strong pedagogical model. Recent research on educational game design and development therefore emphasizes that effective products must align instructional goals, interaction design, analytics, accessibility, and iterative evaluation rather than focusing on game mechanics alone [7][8][18][19].
      There are also important risks and constraints that must be addressed during development. The novelty effect may cause early excitement that fades if the application does not evolve over time. Excessive dependence on points or rankings may discourage some learners instead of motivating them. Data-driven personalization introduces questions of transparency, fairness, privacy, and human oversight, especially when AI is involved in adaptation or feedback generation. International guidance on AI in education stresses that educational systems must remain accountable, age-appropriate, and ethically governed, with clear limits on data use and strong attention to learner safety [1][7][20].
      Taken together, these studies reveal an important research and development gap. A large body of literature evaluates particular tools, particular interventions, or particular effects of gamification and digital educational games. Another body of literature studies adaptive learning, intelligent tutoring systems, learning analytics, and AI in education. Yet fewer works translate these strands into the concrete development of an integrated application that combines game-based learning, adaptive intelligence, embedded analytics, and user-centered product design within one coherent software artifact. This gap provides a strong rationale for the present thesis. The project is therefore positioned not only as an educational technology study, but also as a software engineering task: to design and develop an application whose features are directly grounded in the best available research on AI-enhanced game-based learning [10][11][12][17][18][19].
Research Aim
The aim of this thesis is to design and develop a prototype application for AI-enhanced game-based learning that combines game-based interaction, adaptive support, personalized feedback, and learning analytics in a single educational platform.
Research Objectives
To achieve this aim, the thesis addresses the following objectives:
• To identify the pedagogical, technical, and ethical requirements of an AI-enhanced game-based learning application on the basis of current academic literature.
• To design the system architecture, user roles, interface logic, and core workflows of the application, including student interaction, adaptive task flow, feedback mechanisms, and teacher-facing analytics.
• To implement a functional prototype that includes essential features such as user profiles, game-based learning activities, progress tracking, adaptive or AI-supported feedback, and configurable motivational mechanics.
• To conduct a preliminary evaluation of the prototype in terms of usability, perceived engagement, and the educational relevance of its AI-enhanced features, using an appropriate validation approach such as expert review, pilot testing, or structured user feedback.
Conclusion
      In conclusion, the topic "Development of an Application for AI-Enhanced Game-Based Learning" is academically and practically justified by the convergence of several established research directions: gamification, digital game-based learning, adaptive educational systems, intelligent tutoring, learning analytics, and responsible AI in education. Existing studies show that game-based and gamified environments can improve learner engagement and, when properly designed, support stronger learning processes. At the same time, the literature demonstrates that these benefits are not automatic; they depend on personalization, sound pedagogy, meaningful feedback, careful UX design, and ethical use of learner data. For this reason, the proposed thesis does not treat gamification as a superficial motivational layer. Instead, it approaches application development as the creation of an integrated educational system in which AI functions as a support layer for adaptation, analytics, and feedback, while game-based interaction provides the experiential structure through which learning becomes more active, motivating, and responsive to individual learner needs. This makes the project relevant both as a diploma application in software development and as a research-grounded contribution to contemporary educational technology.
