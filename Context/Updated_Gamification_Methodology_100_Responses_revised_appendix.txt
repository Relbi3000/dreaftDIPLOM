Gamification in Education: Updated Survey Analysis of Student Engagement and Learning Outcomes
Updated methodology and results based on 100 survey responses
Group SE-2326: Bimurzin Assanali, Telgarina Kuralay, Baimurzin Bakhtiyar
Survey instrument (Google Forms): open link

Dataset summary: 100 total respondents for the closed-ended survey; the first two open-ended prompts also received 100 responses each, while the final improvement question received 81 responses.


Introduction
Gamified learning platforms integrate elements such as points, badges, levels, leaderboards, timers, and immediate feedback into learning activities. These tools are increasingly common in both school and university settings because they can make routine practice feel more interactive, competitive, and enjoyable. Platforms such as Kahoot!, Quizizz, and Quizlet are often used to increase attention, participation, and short-cycle practice during lessons.
The underlying idea of gamification is not simply to entertain students, but to align game mechanics with educational goals. When designed well, these mechanics can encourage repetition, persistence, and quick correction of mistakes. At the same time, research and classroom experience suggest that gamification is most effective when it supports learning rather than replacing it. Competition, time pressure, and rewards can motivate some students, but they may also distract others if the activity becomes too focused on winning rather than understanding.
Purpose and Research Focus
The purpose of this updated study was to examine how students perceive gamified learning after the survey dataset expanded to 100 respondents. The revised version updates the participant profile, descriptive statistics, and qualitative analysis so that the methodology and results reflect the larger sample rather than the earlier smaller dataset.
The study addressed two main questions:
• Does the use of gamified platforms increase student engagement and participation?
• Do students believe these platforms improve their learning, memory, and assessment performance?
By combining quantitative survey results with open-ended responses, the report aims to identify which gamification elements students value most, which elements create tension or mixed reactions, and what changes students would make to improve classroom use.
Methodology
Survey Design
A cross-sectional survey was used to collect student perceptions of gamified learning. The questionnaire included four parts: (a) demographic information, (b) exposure to gamified platforms and common features, (c) Likert-scale statements on engagement and perceived learning outcomes, and (d) open-ended questions asking students to describe helpful features, concrete learning experiences, and suggested improvements.
Each attitudinal statement used a 5-point Likert scale ranging from 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree). The updated report keeps the same instrument logic as the earlier draft, but all narrative interpretation has been recalculated for the expanded dataset of 100 respondents.
Participants
A total of 100 respondents completed the closed-ended portion of the survey. The sample was dominated by bachelor-level students and concentrated in the typical university age range, especially 18-20 and 20-22 years old. Participation was voluntary and anonymous, allowing students to answer freely about both positive and negative aspects of gamified learning.
Open-ended participation varied slightly by question. The first prompt on helpful features received 100 responses, the second prompt on memorable learning moments also received 100 responses, and the final prompt on suggested changes received 81 responses. This means the qualitative section combines full-response and partial-response items.
Data Collection
The survey was distributed online through Google Forms. Responses were collected in a format that automatically generated summary charts for the closed-ended questions. These updated Google Forms screenshots were used as the visual basis for the figures embedded in this report. Because the instrument was completed online, students could respond conveniently and with a degree of privacy that may improve candor in self-report data.
Data Analysis
Quantitative data were analyzed using descriptive statistics, primarily frequencies and percentages. The interpretation focuses on overall patterns, such as the proportion of positive responses (Agree + Strongly Agree), neutral responses, and negative responses (Disagree + Strongly Disagree). No inferential statistics were applied because the goal of the study was descriptive rather than causal.
Qualitative responses were reviewed using a light thematic approach. Recurring patterns were grouped into broad themes such as immediate feedback, progress/rewards, competition, repetition/retries, and suggestions for classroom improvement. To keep the report readable, the qualitative appendix presents a condensed thematic coding table with representative excerpts rather than reproducing every raw screenshot page.
Results
Demographics and General Exposure
The updated sample remains strongly university-centered. As shown in Figure 1, 61% of respondents were aged 18-20 and another 32% were aged 20-22. Only a small minority fell outside those ranges, which means the findings mainly reflect the views of typical undergraduate-age students. Gender distribution was moderately imbalanced but still broad enough to reflect mixed classroom perspectives: 58% identified as female and 42% as male. Study level was overwhelmingly bachelor-level (94%), with only a few master's, PhD, and school-student respondents.
Gamified activities were present in university courses, but not usually on a daily basis. Figure 1 also shows that 37% encountered them 1-3 times per month, 31% less than once a month, 21% one to two times per week, and 5% more than three times per week, while 6% reported never encountering them. This suggests that gamification is already familiar to most students, but its use is still periodic rather than fully integrated into everyday instruction.

Figure 1. Participant profile: age, gender, study level, and frequency of gamified-tool use.
Figure 2 shows which platforms and mechanics students encounter most often. Kahoot! was by far the most familiar platform (81%), followed by Quizizz (64%) and Quizlet (56%). Most students associated gamification with live, timed, whole-class activities during lectures or seminars (63%), while self-paced practice accounted for 17% and a balanced mix for 16%. The most commonly seen mechanics were points/badges/levels (73%), public leaderboards (60%), and timers or speed bonuses (49%).
The exposure pattern is important because it explains the rest of the results: many students are primarily experiencing gamification through fast-paced classroom quizzes rather than through slower, mastery-based systems.

Figure 2. Exposure to gamified tools, usage mode, and commonly encountered features.
Engagement, Attention, and Learning Outcomes
The central Likert-scale results were generally positive. As Figure 3 indicates, 56% of students agreed or strongly agreed that they pay more attention during gamified activities, and 67% agreed or strongly agreed that they participate more actively when such tools are used. Immediate feedback stood out as especially valuable: 69% agreed or strongly agreed that it helps them understand mistakes.
The effect of competition-based mechanics was more mixed. Public leaderboards motivated 55% of respondents, but 25% were neutral and 20% negative. Timers produced the most divided pattern: only 43% agreed that timers help them focus, while 33% disagreed and 24% stayed neutral. This indicates that time pressure can energize some students but can also create stress or reduce depth of thinking for others.
Perceived learning outcomes were still mostly favorable. Sixty-five percent agreed or strongly agreed that they remember course content better after using these tools, and 55% felt their assessment performance improved, although 29% were neutral on the performance question. Taken together, the data suggest that students are more confident about gamification's influence on engagement and memory than on measurable assessment gains.

Figure 3. Student ratings of engagement and perceived learning outcomes on the 5-point Likert scale.
Preferred Feature Design
When students rated specific mechanics directly, the most positively viewed options were self-paced mode (66% positive), live whole-class mode (64%), points/badges/levels (64%), and the ability to retry items (62%). Immediate feedback with explanations also remained strong at 59% positive, which reinforces the idea that gamification works best when it supports error correction and repeated practice.
Again, speed-based mechanics were more controversial than supportive. Timers and speed bonuses received only 56% positive ratings and also attracted a relatively high share of negative responses. This pattern fits the qualitative comments, where students often said they liked challenge and competition, but not when speed became more important than understanding.

Figure 4. Perceived usefulness of specific gamification features.
Leaderboard preferences were also nuanced. Figure 5 shows that 40% said either public or private ranking is fine, 38% preferred a public class leaderboard, 20% preferred private progress only, and just 2% rejected leaderboards entirely. This means students do not uniformly oppose competition, but many want flexibility in how visible that competition is.
A separate comparison question also showed that 41% spend more focused time on the task during gamified activities than in regular lessons, 29% spend about the same amount of time, 23% spend less focused time, and 7% were not sure. This again points to a positive overall effect, but not a universal one.

Qualitative Insights from Open-Ended Responses
The open-ended responses add important detail to the numerical patterns. Four recurring themes were especially visible across the screenshots.
Immediate feedback as the strongest learning support. Students repeatedly said that instant correction helps them understand mistakes on the spot. Several responses emphasized that seeing the correct answer immediately, or receiving a short explanation after an error, makes learning faster and more memorable. In other words, feedback was not just motivating; it was instructional.
Progress, points, and rewards as motivational structure. Many students connected learning with visible progress markers such as points, levels, badges, and progress bars. These features were described as making learning feel structured, encouraging persistence, and reducing boredom. Respondents often used phrases such as "seeing my progress" or "leveling up," which suggests that progress visibility matters as much as the reward itself.
Competition and challenge help-but only up to a point. A number of students said competition pushes them to focus, especially in platforms like Kahoot! and Quizizz. They mentioned leaderboards, time pressure, and the wish to beat their previous score. However, other comments warned that too much emphasis on speed can shift attention away from understanding. This helps explain why timers and leaderboards received more mixed ratings than feedback and retries.
Repetition, retries, and active recall improve memory. Students frequently referred to flashcards, repeated questions, retries, hints, and pre-exam quiz sessions as helpful because they force active recall. Examples included learning vocabulary, grammar, formulas, history dates, and programming concepts. The common pattern was that gamified tools help most when they create short cycles of practice, correction, and repetition.
The final open-ended question, answered by 81 students, focused on how teachers could improve their use of these tools. The most common suggestions were to add explanations after each question, reduce speed pressure, use gamified tools more often but more purposefully, include more team-based activities, and personalize difficulty so that students remain challenged without feeling overwhelmed.
These comments are especially important because they show that students are not asking for "more game" in a superficial sense. Instead, they are asking for better instructional design: more debriefing, more learning-oriented feedback, less guessing for points, and a better balance between competition and mastery.
Discussion
The updated dataset strengthens the original interpretation rather than reversing it. Across 100 respondents, gamification appears to be a broadly positive support for attention, participation, and short-term learning confidence. Students clearly associate gamified activities with higher energy, more active recall, and better awareness of mistakes.
At the same time, the larger sample also makes the limits of gamification more visible. Not every student experiences competition in the same way, and speed-based design remains the clearest source of mixed reactions. The results therefore support a balanced model: gamification works best when it combines motivation with explanatory feedback, retries, and meaningful practice rather than relying only on ranking and time pressure.
Another important finding is that students are comfortable with multiple delivery formats. Self-paced mode and live whole-class mode both received strong support, which suggests that teachers do not need a single rigid template. Instead, they can adapt the format to the lesson goal-for example, fast group reviews before an exam, or slower self-paced practice for consolidation and homework.
Limitations
This study has several limitations. First, it relies on self-reported perception rather than direct performance data, so students' impressions of improved learning may not always match measured achievement. Second, the sample is a convenience sample rather than a random one, which limits generalizability. Third, the results largely reflect university students-especially bachelor-level respondents-so they should be extended to other populations with caution.
In addition, the open-ended responses were analyzed from Google Forms screenshots rather than from a fully exported spreadsheet. For that reason, the qualitative coding should be read as descriptive and illustrative rather than as a line-by-line exhaustive coding of every response.
Conclusion
The revised survey analysis based on 100 responses confirms that gamified learning is widely viewed as beneficial for student engagement. Most respondents reported paying more attention, participating more actively, and remembering content better when gamified tools were used.
However, the findings also make clear that not all game mechanics are equally effective. Immediate feedback, retries, progress tracking, points, and flexible pacing were the most consistently valued features. By contrast, timers and speed-focused competition produced more divided reactions.
Overall, the updated methodology and results suggest that gamification is most effective when it is used as a structured learning aid rather than as entertainment alone. Students respond best when the activity helps them practice, understand mistakes quickly, track improvement, and reflect on answers after the game. For teachers, the implication is straightforward: design gamified activities around learning goals first, and use competition only when it supports-not replaces-understanding.
