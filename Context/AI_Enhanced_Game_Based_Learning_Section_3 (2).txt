Section 3. Analysis of Existing Systems and Conceptual Framework
Topic: Development of an Application for AI-Enhanced Game-Based Learning
The current landscape of AI-enhanced game-based learning shows a clear movement away from simple gamified quizzes and toward adaptive, data-driven, and feedback-rich educational environments. Recent research identifies adaptation, personalization, and AI-supported content generation as major directions in this field, while studies on adaptive learning technologies stress that classroom usability, teacher acceptance, and pedagogical alignment are just as important as technical innovation (Chiotaki et al., 2023; Simon & Zeng, 2024). In parallel, recent evidence on intelligent tutoring systems suggests that these systems can improve learning attitudes and test scores, although their effects vary by design, context, and learner characteristics (Huang et al., 2025).
3.1 Evaluation of Existing Systems
A review of current systems relevant to AI-enhanced game-based learning highlights four useful reference points for the proposed application: Kahoot!, Wayground AI, Duolingo Max, and EduXgame. These solutions illustrate different approaches to AI support, gamification, feedback design, and educational scope.
SystemCore AI / GBL FeaturesStrengthsLimitationsKahoot!AI-generated quizzes from topics, PDFs, URLs, and articles; strong live gamified interaction.Fast content authoring, high classroom engagement, simple teacher workflow.Mainly session-based; limited long-term learner modelling and adaptive progression.Wayground AIAI-generated assessments from documents, prompts, websites, videos, curricula, and images.Flexible assessment creation, differentiation support, accessibility features, teacher-facing analytics.AI outputs require teacher review; emphasis remains on assessment generation rather than deep learner adaptation.Duolingo MaxGenerative AI for roleplay, video call, conversational practice, and feedback.Strong interactive feedback loop, high engagement, good example of AI-supported practice.Focused primarily on language learning; not designed as a general multi-subject school platform.EduXgameResearch prototype with AI-driven content generation, quizzes, flashcards, and matching games.Close to the proposed thesis topic; links teacher uploads with gamified learning material generation.Prototype-stage evidence; limited information on large-scale implementation and long-term classroom adoption.Kahoot! is particularly effective for rapid content authoring and short-term motivation because it allows teachers to generate activities from topics, PDFs, webpages, and other source material. However, its core strength remains live quiz interaction rather than sustained individualized learning pathways (Kahoot!, 2026).
Wayground AI extends this model by offering broader AI-assisted assessment design. It supports generation from multiple input types and provides tools that help teachers adapt, simplify, and differentiate content. Its main advantage is flexibility, but the platform itself notes that AI-generated material should be reviewed before publication, which means teacher oversight remains essential (Wayground, 2025a, 2026).
Duolingo Max demonstrates the value of combining gamification with generative AI feedback. Its roleplay and video call functions provide learners with scenario-based interaction and immediate response opportunities, which is highly relevant for designing engaging educational applications. Nevertheless, it is a domain-specific system built for language learning rather than a general-purpose educational platform (Duolingo, 2024).
EduXgame is especially relevant because it is an open-access academic prototype developed for secondary education. It integrates AI-driven content generation with game-based learning tools such as quizzes, flashcards, and matching activities. This makes it conceptually close to the present thesis topic, although published evidence about its scalability and long-term use is still limited (Khaoula et al., 2025).
3.2 Conceptual Framework
Based on the reviewed literature and benchmark systems, the proposed application should be guided by a conceptual framework that combines four interdependent components: (1) learner modelling, (2) adaptive game mechanics, (3) AI-assisted content and feedback generation, and (4) teacher oversight.
Framework ComponentRole in the Proposed ApplicationRationaleLearner modellingTracks performance, progress, and response patterns.Provides the basis for personalization and adaptive task selection.Adaptive game mechanicsAdjusts difficulty, progression, rewards, and challenge level.Keeps the experience motivating while aligning gameplay with learning goals.AI-assisted generation and feedbackCreates tasks, hints, explanations, and feedback in response to learner needs.Supports scalability, immediacy, and variation in content delivery.Teacher oversightAllows teachers to review, correct, and align content with curricular objectives.Reduces the risks of AI inaccuracy and improves classroom relevance and trust.This framework reflects the literature on adaptive game-based learning, which shows that personalization and adaptation are central design priorities in educational games (Chiotaki et al., 2023). It also reflects broader evidence that intelligent tutoring systems tend to be most effective when feedback, task selection, and support are closely matched to learner needs (Huang et al., 2025). At the same time, teacher perspectives must remain central, because successful adoption depends on usability, time demands, and institutional support (Simon & Zeng, 2024).
3.3 SWOT Analysis of the Proposed Project
DimensionAnalysisStrengthsThe proposed application can integrate AI-generated content, adaptive progression, gamified interaction, and explanatory feedback within one system. This offers a more unified learner experience than tools that focus only on quiz generation or only on conversational practice.WeaknessesDeveloping a system that is both engaging and academically rigorous is complex. Gamification alone does not guarantee learning improvement, and AI-generated material may require validation and teacher review.OpportunitiesThere is a strong opportunity to build a multi-subject platform that combines the best elements of current systems: rapid authoring, adaptive support, feedback-rich interaction, and curriculum alignment.ThreatsKey threats include AI inaccuracies, teacher resistance caused by workload or limited training, and weak institutional adoption if the system is seen as entertaining but not pedagogically reliable.3.4 How the Proposed Project Improves on Existing Systems
The proposed project improves on existing systems in three important ways. First, unlike tools that mainly assist with content or quiz generation, the new application is designed around an adaptive learner-centred loop in which performance influences subsequent tasks, difficulty level, and feedback. Second, unlike Duolingo Max, which is highly effective but domain-specific, the proposed application is intended to support broader educational contexts rather than only language learning. Third, unlike early research prototypes, the project explicitly balances AI capability with teacher control, explainable feedback, and alignment with curricular objectives. As a result, the application aims to move beyond isolated AI features toward a more complete and classroom-relevant AI-enhanced game-based learning system.
3.5 Methods and Sources Used to Collect Data for the Project
Data for the project should be collected through a mixed-source approach. First, a desk-based review of open-access journal articles, systematic reviews, and meta-analyses should be used to identify best practices in adaptive learning, game-based learning, and intelligent tutoring. Second, a comparative review of existing systems should be conducted using official product documentation, feature pages, and public descriptions for tools such as Kahoot!, Wayground AI, and Duolingo Max. Third, user requirement data should be gathered from the target audience through questionnaires, interviews, or focus groups with students and teachers. Finally, after prototype development, pilot usability testing and system log data should be used to evaluate engagement, completion, user satisfaction, and the quality of adaptive responses. This multi-source strategy is appropriate because both the technical and human dimensions of adoption are critical in adaptive educational technologies (Simon & Zeng, 2024).
3.6 Challenges and Limitations in Obtaining Relevant Data
Several challenges may affect data collection for the project. A first limitation is the cold-start problem: because the system is new, there may be insufficient learner data at the beginning to support robust personalization. A second challenge is access to school-based participants, since work with students often requires ethics approval, institutional permission, and informed consent procedures. A third challenge concerns teacher participation, because adaptive systems may be perceived as time-consuming or technically demanding. Finally, AI-generated educational content cannot be accepted without validation. Commercial platforms themselves acknowledge that generated outputs may contain inaccuracies, which means that quality assurance through teacher or expert review is likely to be necessary during early development and testing phases (Wayground, 2025a).
